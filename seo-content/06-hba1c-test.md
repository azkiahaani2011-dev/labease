## What Is HbA1c?

**HbA1c** stands for **Haemoglobin A1c** (also called Glycated Haemoglobin or Glycosylated Haemoglobin).

Here is how it works in simple terms:

- Sugar (glucose) in your blood naturally sticks to haemoglobin (the protein in red blood cells)
- The more glucose in your blood, the more it sticks
- Red blood cells live for about **90–120 days**
- So HbA1c measures how much sugar has been stuck to your red blood cells over the past **2–3 months**

A single fasting blood sugar test tells you what your sugar is *right now*. HbA1c tells you what it has been *on average for 3 months* — which is far more useful for diagnosing and managing diabetes.

## HbA1c Normal Range

| HbA1c Level | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| Below 5.7% | Normal — No diabetes |
| 5.7% – 6.4% | Prediabetes — At risk, lifestyle changes needed |
| 6.5% and above | Diabetes — Confirmed diagnosis |
| Below 7.0% (in diabetics) | Well-controlled diabetes |
| 7.0% – 8.0% (in diabetics) | Acceptable control |
| Above 8.0% (in diabetics) | Poor control — medication or lifestyle adjustment needed |
| Above 10% | Very high risk of complications — urgent medical attention |

> ⚠️ HbA1c targets vary by individual. Older adults, pregnant women, and those with kidney disease may have different targets. Always follow your doctor's specific advice.

## Why Should You Get an HbA1c Test?

### For Diagnosis
- You have symptoms of diabetes (frequent thirst, frequent urination, blurry vision, fatigue)
- You are overweight or obese
- You have a family history of Type 2 diabetes
- Your previous fasting blood sugar was borderline

### For Monitoring (Already Diagnosed)
- Check how well your diabetes is controlled
- Assess if your current medication is working
- Guide dose adjustments by your doctor

### For Prevention (Prediabetes)
- Know if you are on the path to diabetes before it develops
- Take early lifestyle action (diet, exercise, weight loss)

### Annual Screening (Recommended For)
- All adults above 45 years
- Overweight or obese individuals of any age
- Women who had gestational diabetes
- People with PCOS or thyroid disorders
- People with high blood pressure or cholesterol

## What Does a High HbA1c Mean?

An HbA1c of **6.5% or above** confirms diabetes.

Uncontrolled high HbA1c over time causes:
- **Retinopathy** — damage to the eyes (can lead to blindness)
- **Nephropathy** — kidney damage (can lead to kidney failure)
- **Neuropathy** — nerve damage (numbness, tingling in hands/feet)
- **Cardiovascular disease** — heart attacks, strokes
- **Poor wound healing** — infections and gangrene

**Reducing HbA1c by even 1%** significantly reduces the risk of all these complications.

## How to Lower Your HbA1c Naturally

If your HbA1c is in the prediabetes or mildly elevated range, these lifestyle changes can make a real difference:

### Diet Changes
- Reduce refined carbohydrates (white rice, white bread, maida)
- Eat more fibre (vegetables, whole grains, lentils)
- Avoid sugary drinks (cold drinks, fruit juices, sweetened tea)
- Eat smaller, more frequent meals
- Add protein to every meal (eggs, dal, paneer, fish, chicken)

### Exercise
- **30 minutes of brisk walking daily** can lower HbA1c by 0.5–1%
- Resistance training (weights, squats) improves insulin sensitivity
- Even breaking up sitting every hour helps

### Weight Loss
- Losing just **5–10% of body weight** can significantly improve HbA1c
- For a 70 kg person, that is only 3.5–7 kg

### Sleep & Stress
- Poor sleep raises blood sugar — aim for 7–8 hours
- Chronic stress increases cortisol, which raises blood sugar
- Meditation, yoga, and deep breathing help manage stress

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)

### What is HbA1c test used for?
HbA1c (Glycated Haemoglobin) is used to diagnose diabetes and prediabetes, and to monitor how well blood sugar is controlled in diabetic patients over the past 2–3 months. It is more reliable than a fasting blood sugar test because it reflects long-term average blood glucose, not just a single moment.

### What is a normal HbA1c level?
Normal HbA1c is **below 5.7%**. A level of 5.7–6.4% indicates prediabetes. 6.5% and above confirms Type 2 diabetes. For people already on diabetes treatment, a target below 7.0% is generally recommended by doctors.

### Does HbA1c test require fasting?
No. HbA1c does not require fasting. You can eat and drink normally before the test. This is one major advantage over fasting blood sugar tests.

### How often should I get HbA1c tested?
If you have diabetes, the standard recommendation is every **3 months** to track control. If well controlled (HbA1c consistently below 7%), your doctor may reduce this to every 6 months. For prediabetes screening, once a year is advisable.

### Can HbA1c be high without diabetes?
Yes, in some conditions. Iron deficiency anaemia, haemolytic anaemia, kidney disease, and certain haemoglobin variants can affect HbA1c readings. Your doctor will consider these factors when interpreting results.

### What is a dangerous HbA1c level?
An HbA1c above 10% is considered very high and indicates poorly controlled diabetes with serious risk of complications. An HbA1c above 12–13% may require hospitalisation. Urgent medical attention is needed.

### How quickly can HbA1c be reduced?
HbA1c reflects 3 months of blood sugar. With strict diet, exercise, and medication, you can expect a reduction of 1–2% within 3 months. Sustainable lifestyle changes are more effective long-term than crash diets.

### Is HbA1c the same as blood sugar?
No. Blood sugar (glucose) is measured in mg/dL and reflects your sugar level at a specific moment. HbA1c is expressed as a percentage and reflects your average sugar level over 2–3 months. Both are useful — HbA1c is better for diagnosis and monitoring, while blood glucose is useful for daily self-monitoring.
